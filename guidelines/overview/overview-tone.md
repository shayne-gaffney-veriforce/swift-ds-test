# Content and Voice

All UI text should sound like it comes from the same knowledgeable, trustworthy colleague. We help people manage contractor safety and compliance — the stakes are real, and our voice reflects that.

---

## Voice Principles

**Knowledgeable and confident**
We set industry standards and are candid about impact.
- Be concise, consistent, reliable, informative
- Don't be egocentric, verbose, or pedantic

**Friendly yet mature**
We take each relationship seriously.
- Be kind, helpful, and also serious
- Don't be playful, humorous, overly familiar, or monotone

**Transparent and humble**
We own our mistakes and stay honest.
- Be clear, direct, and honest
- Don't oversell, use jargon, deceive, or be grandiose

---

## Writing Rules

- Be direct — say what you mean in as few words as possible
- Lead with the action — tell users what they can do, not what they can't
- Use active voice — "You saved the form" not "The form was saved"
- Offer solutions — when something goes wrong, explain how to fix it
- Skip the jargon — write for a person, not a compliance manual
- Be consistent — use the same word for the same thing everywhere

---

## Capitalization

- Headings: Sentence case ("Contractor details")
- Buttons: Title Case ("Save Changes")
- Labels: Sentence case ("Email address")
- Navigation: Sentence case ("Settings")
- Acronyms: All caps (OSHA, DOT, ISN)

---

## Punctuation

- No periods on buttons, labels, headings, or toast messages
- Use periods in body text, descriptions, and helper text
- Use contractions — "you're," "can't," "won't"
- Skip exclamation points — one per page maximum, only for genuine celebration
- Use serial commas — "safety, compliance, and training"

---

## Microcopy Patterns

### Buttons

Lead with a verb. Be specific about what happens.

- "Save Changes" not "Submit"
- "Add Contractor" not "Add"
- "Send Invitation" not "OK"
- "Remove Certificate" not "Yes"

Button pairs: Primary action should be specific, secondary can be generic.
- "Save Changes" / "Cancel" — not "Yes" / "No"

### Form Labels

Be concise. Don't repeat the field type in the label.

- "Email" not "Email address field"
- "Phone" not "Enter your phone number"

Mark optional fields "(optional)" rather than marking required fields.

### Helper Text

Explain format requirements or provide useful context. Don't repeat the label.

- "We'll send a verification link to this address"
- "10 digits, no dashes"
- "This cannot be changed later"

### Empty States

Include what this area is for, plus one clear action to get started.

- "No contractors yet. Add your first contractor to get started."
- "No results for '[query]'. Try adjusting your filters."
- "You're all caught up."

Tone: Neutral and helpful, not apologetic. Don't say "Oops!" or "Sorry!"

### Error Messages

Include what went wrong and how to fix it, in plain language.

- "That email is already registered. Try signing in instead."
- "Enter a valid phone number (10 digits)"
- "Certificate expired on Jun 10, 2025. Upload a current version."
- "Connection lost. Check your internet and try again."

Never show raw error codes like "Error 503" or "Validation failed."

Show errors inline, next to the field that needs attention.

### Success Messages

Brief confirmation that the action worked. No need to over-celebrate.

- "Changes saved" — not "Success! Your changes have been saved successfully!"
- "Invitation sent" — not "Great job! The invitation is on its way!"

### Confirmation Dialogs

Use for destructive or irreversible actions. State the consequence clearly.

- Title: Action being confirmed ("Remove this contractor?")
- Body: What will happen ("This will permanently remove Alex Chen and all associated records.")
- Buttons: Specific action + Cancel ("Remove Contractor" / "Cancel")

### Tooltips

One sentence max. Explain what something does, not what it is.

- "Filter by compliance status" — not "This is the compliance filter"

### Loading States

- Quick action: Spinner only, no text
- 2-5 seconds: "Saving..."
- Longer process: "Uploading certificate... This may take a minute."

## Terminology

Use consistent terms throughout the product.

- Contractor (not vendor, supplier, subcontractor)
- Certificate (not certification, cert, credential)
- Sign in / Sign out (not log in / log out)
- Email (not e-mail)
- Set up as verb, setup as noun
